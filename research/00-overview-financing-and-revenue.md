## Part 1 — How indie game development is financed

The core tension: good game dev needs sustained deep focus, which is hard to
sustain on a side-hustle schedule. People solve it with a *stack* of sources,
rarely just one.

### Self-funding / runway (most common)
- **Save up first** — bank 1–3 years of living expenses, live cheap, then go
  full-time.
- **Partner/family income** quietly subsidizes most early studios.

### Keep earning, but structure it
- **Contract/freelance in chunks** — work 3 months, dev 3 months.
- **Part-time / 4-day weeks** to preserve creative energy.
- **Co-founder split** — one earns while the other builds.

### External funding
- **Publishers** (Devolver, Raw Fury, Annapurna, Hooded Horse…) fund dev for a
  revenue cut; usually need a polished **vertical slice** to pitch.
- **Platform funding** — Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo, Epic MegaGrants; advances
  for exclusivity.
- **Government grants & tax credits** — Canada, UK, France, Nordics, Australia.
  Underrated; sometimes free money for paperwork.
- **Angels / VC** — rare for single games, more for studios/live-service.

### Community / pre-revenue
- **Crowdfunding** (Kickstarter) — funds dev *and* validates demand.
- **Steam Early Access** — sell a playable-but-unfinished game and fund the back
  half of development. Now arguably the dominant modern indie model
  (*Hades*, *Baldur's Gate 3*, *Valheim*).
- **Patreon / itch.io / demos** to build audience + recurring income.

### Honest reality
Most successful indies combine *savings + a grant + Early Access*, or
*publisher advance + tax credit + wishlists from a demo*. The two
highest-leverage moves: **build a vertical slice while still employed**, and
**research grants/tax credits for your region**.

## Part 2 — Earning without building (or shipping) a full game

Two buckets: **build once → resell forever** (assets/tools), and **piecework for
other developers** (freelance/contract).

### Sell reusable assets
- Unity Asset Store / Unreal **Fab**, itch.io asset packs, music & SFX, 3D models.
- Code/tools tend to out-earn art because there's less competition.

### Freelance / contract piecework
- Art, audio, **code contracting** (gameplay systems, porting, shaders, tools),
  narrative/localization.
- Better rates come from visibility (Twitter/Bluesky, r/INAT,
  r/gameDevClassifieds, Discords, jams) than from Fiverr/Upwork.

### Content & community
- Tutorials/courses, devlogs, tooling newsletters (monetize teaching, not games).

### Plugins & tools for developers (best effort→recurring ratio)
- Editor extensions, importers, debugging/automation tools for
  Unity/Unreal/Godot. Sell on Fab / itch / Gumroad / Ko-fi.

### Game jams & bounties
- Jams build portfolio + collaborators; open engines (Godot, O3DE) sometimes
  have funded bounties.

### Why C++ is a strong hand here
The asset-store world is flooded with artists; **native C++ talent is scarce**
because Unreal gameplay devs lean on Blueprint and Unity devs live in C#. The
people who can write real engine-level C++ are a small pool — several have
turned single plugins into actual businesses. The C++-specific, demand-backed
detail is in the deep-research file.
